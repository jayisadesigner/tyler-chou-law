# The Hidden Cost of "Exposure" Deals

Let's talk about the four-letter word of the creator economy: free.

"We can't pay you, but think of the exposure!" It's a tale as old as YouTube itself. And look, I get it—early in your career, visibility matters. But here's what nobody tells you: exposure deals set a precedent that's incredibly hard to undo.

When you work for free, you're not just giving away your time. You're establishing your market rate. You're telling that brand—and everyone they talk to—that your work doesn't require compensation. That follows you.

## The Math Nobody Does

Let's say a brand offers you an "exposure" deal that would take 20 hours of your time. If your rate should be $500/hour (and for many established creators, it should be higher), you just gave away $10,000.

For what? A logo on a website? A shoutout to an audience that may or may not convert?

## What to Say Instead

Here's a script I give my clients:

> "I appreciate you thinking of me for this. My standard rate for this type of work is [X]. I'm happy to discuss a reduced rate for the right partnership, but I'm not in a position to work for exposure alone. What budget do you have available?"

That's it. Professional, clear, leaves the door open.

## The Exception

Is there ever a time to work for free? Maybe. If it's a cause you genuinely believe in, a friend you want to help, or a creative project that feeds your soul—go for it. But make that choice consciously, not because someone convinced you that exposure pays rent.

It doesn't.
